Unstitched: So-Le Studio | sustainable jewellery

 Posted on April 1, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  979 words  |  Reinaldo Massengill

When it comes to sustainable fashion, it can be hard to know where to start. In light of this, every month we will focus on a brand that knows exactly what it means to be a sustainable force for good in fashion today.

This month we’re getting to know So-Le Studio, a sustainable jewellery brand that transforms leather off-cuts and metal shavings into unique pieces.

One of the most sustainable ways we can consume newness is by upcycling and making use of what we already have. This is the basis of what formed Maria Sole Ferragamo's conscious jewellery brand So-Le Studio, which only launched in 2019 and has since then achieved great things. As one of four grandchildren of legendary Italian designer Salvatore Ferragamo, it seemed only natural that she ended up pursuing a career in fashion and design.

Courtesy of So-Le Studio

Maria Sole Ferragamo

The brand was born in London but is now based in Milan, with production remaining close in Florence's picturesque hills. Here, discarded materials are reborn into one-of-a-kind pieces for all generations to wear and love forever. Ferragamo finds scrap leather off-cuts and other surplus materials across Italian warehouses and gives them a new lease of life under her brand.

So-Le Studio releases one collection per year, showcasing new designs that are instantly recognisable as her own, thanks to its eye-catching shapes like earrings that curl around the ear or oversized draping necklaces. Her artistic approach to jewellery steps from her years studying architecture at Politecnico di Milano, followed by her masters in jewellery from London's Central Saint Martins.

Now couldn't be a more exciting time for the designer, as she's just launched her latest collections at Browns and continues to carve her space in the sustainable jewellery market – Bazaar gets to know both the woman behind the brand, why upcycling is so vital for a greener fashion industry and how shoppers can consume in a more conscious way.

Courtesy of So-Le Studio

What makes a truly sustainable brand?

Maria Sole Ferragamo: "The most important aspect to consider is the mindset behind the business; being conscious about each decision and action you make and its consequences.

"I don’t think there is ever a point of arrival when you speak about sustainability, but a constant strive towards a limitless horizon. To successfully run a sustainable business, you need to be agile and push the boundaries, understand innovations and strive for your own."

How can jewellers, in particular, be more responsible?

"I personally prefer to use existing material. Jewellery is a beautiful field as it's more timeless, has longevity and is less dependent on trends. There are many beautiful materials available that can be recycled again and as you can see with So-Le Studio it doesn't always have to be gold or precious materials."

Courtesy of So-Le Studio

What do you think needs to change in the industry?

"I feel the industry is becoming more and more aware of the urgency of the world. This is a development also of consumer demands as they are more often speaking about sustainability nowadays. What should happen more is education in greenwashing, as the word [sustainability] is now overused."

What do you want to achieve personally with your brand, in terms of sustainability?

"I want to create pieces that celebrate beauty, longevity, and innovation. Collections that challenge the concept of value as something related to the preciousness of the material and show how an abandoned resource can be transformed and honoured. I find great joy in giving the materials I find a new life."

What advice would you give to those wanting to make their business sustainable?

"First of all, define what sustainability actually means for you and your business, then make choices and actions to move towards the direction you have identified. Keep pushing the boundaries; becoming sustainable is about the journey and a constant evolvement. It's about remaining curious and open-minded and ultimately finding solutions."

What’s the smallest change a consumer could make to become more eco-conscious?

"Before any purchase, you should interrogate yourself about the longevity of it and its meaning for you. How many times are you going to wear this product? How much will you care about it? At the same time, you should think of the practical aspects of it. Where has it been made? By whom? Under which conditions? What’s its impact on the environment? You should never stop at the surface."

What are the most common inaccurate “facts” about sustainability you see pushed out?

"The thinking that you can just tick a box and you're done. I don’t think that this is possible when you speak about sustainability. For instance, saying that a garment is made with natural fibre should not allow anyone to state that the garment is truly sustainable. Where has this natural fibre been produced? What’s the impact of its production on the environment? What’s the worker’s wage? What’s the longevity of that fabric? And so on. It’s important the consumer educates themselves and understands the impact of the products they purchase."

What’s the biggest misconception about sustainable clothing and what would you say to counteract this?

"I feel that more and more we are gaining proof that cool products can be created while taking their impact on the environment and society into consideration. The belief that eco means aesthetically boring or unexciting is slowly being surpassed by brands that create cool and desirable products with a major consciousness behind them."

Courtesy of So-Le Studio

Where do you turn to when you feel confused about sustainability and need more detail?

"Usually to the brand itself. I always investigate where a product has been made, where the materials come from, and the quality and durability I am buying into."
