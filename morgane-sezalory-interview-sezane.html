Morgane Sezalory interview - how I founded Sezane

 Posted on April 8, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  917 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Most of us will have heard of Nasty Gal, the online retailer founded by Sophia Amoruso who originally launched her company by selling vintage clothes from her eBay account. Now imagine a French version. Replace Amoruso with Morgane Sezalory and you have an understated Gallic equivalent. Sezalory started her now hugely popular label Sezane by selling vintage finds on eBay and, in a matter of years, turned it into a lucrative fashion brand whose collections sell out within days.

"My sister was moving to London and left three bags of clothes," Sezalory tells us. "So I decided to sell them – and that was how it started. I'm not sure if she realises that I wouldn't be doing Sezane without her."

At the time, Sezalory had just finished studying and was living in Paris. She knew she wanted to be creative, but had no idea what way. She was juggling part time jobs, but decided to pursue the eBay business, scouring jumble sales, second hand shops and market stalls for vintage finds. Within months, she was supplementing her monthly part-time job salary of €700 with an additional average of £5,000.

"The internet enabled me to invent my own job"

"I wouldn't do a lot to the pieces - maybe switch the buttons, take out shoulder pads or shorten a hem or sleeve," she says. "It was really in the way I took the eBay product picture that changed things. My friends said I could find a rubbish bag and sell it as a dress. I started to grow a following pretty quickly and it made me realise that the internet offered an opportunity. It was an incredible tool that would enable me to invent my own job."

Prompted by a combination of eBay's dated aesthetic and the queues at the post office, Sezalory decided to launch her own platform named Les Composantes. It was a clever, novel idea - every month Sezalory would host an online 'rendezvous' where she'd curate 100 customised vintage pieces to go live at the same time. These would sell out in minutes and served as the push she needed to finally launch her own line, Sezane - a mix of her first and second name. It was around that time that she met her now husband Thibault Lougnon, who also acts as one of two of her business partners.

"He worked as a consultant for young online start-ups," she says. "I remember him saying to me, 'You're mad; you're frustrating your customers by only giving them such a small amount of stock.' But I was worried about hiring staff - I thought if I had employees then I'd have less freedom. But it was Tibault who said, 'There's no freedom in working 24/7 with only a four hours sleep each night.'"

Sezane's spring/summer 2017 collection

And so in 2013 - with the help of business partners Lougnon and Corentin Petit - Sezane was born; vintage-inspired, understated feminine pieces that are easy to wear - from loose silk shirts and flippy floral skirts to embroidered white T-shirts and pretty dresses. Prices were kept relatively accessible - around €80 euros for a dress. What truly marked the brand out from its contemporaries such as Maje and Sandro was its decision to launch as an online store, rather than with a standalone shop. It was, in fact, the first label in France to do so, creating beautifully styled images that shoppers loved.

"A lot of people weren't convinced by the online idea," she recalls. "With the press, it was challenging; fashion can be very exclusive so perhaps me coming in with no formal background, out of nowhere didn't work for them - it just wasn't the way things were then. But my customers came via word of mouth so we grew anyway. And I might not look like a rebel, but I am inside and I knew what I was doing."

Today, Sezane is hugely popular across France with the UK and the US as growing markets. Last year, the brand opened its first bricks and mortar store, L'Appartement in Paris open from Wednesday to Saturday. It's an expansive space which also boasts a mini cinema in its basement (perfect for bored men accompanying their partners shopping), a library next door where customers can read over a coffee, as well as a free photo booth. It's a chic, yet welcoming environment to shop and, on days of new drops, there are queues round the building.

"I always say to my staff the same thing my mum told me when I was young - treat others as you would be treated," she says. "The same goes for hosting - host how you would want to be hosted. It's very important to me that the woman feels well considered and well treated. We want her to feel good in our clothes and in our store."

There's talk of a Sezane pop-up in London, but for now the focus is its forthcoming autumn/winter collection which has been shot in New York. Sezalory has grown with her brand - now 32 and married with two small children, she is a poster child for the potential of the digital world.

"The internet has opened fashion up in a way it never was before," she says. "It used to be so closed - now anyone from any background with a big idea who wants to work hard to make it happen can make a success of themselves."
