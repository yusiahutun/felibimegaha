ABC news anchors were left red-faced yesterday after falling for a prank caller talking 'expertly' about a major water main break on the University of California, Los Angeles campus.

The joker called into ABC 7 in Los Angeles pretending to be a Department of Public Works employee named Louis Slungpue - which he noted should be pronounced 'Slung-poo'.

As live footage of the brown flood waters streamed, Mr Slungpue talked in detail about the rupture to two journalists who failed to realize something was up.

Even when he suggested a 'cherry bomb or a really large dump' as primary causes, the man and woman interviewing him maintained a professional manner.

'That is incredible. So you're 100per cent sure about that?' the female host asked Mr Slungpue, genuinely concerned.

But at that point the prankster hung up and the line went dead.

'Oh we lost him . . . We hope to get him back,' the female anchor informed viewers.

Mr Slungpue first told the news station that the break was caused by college students flushing a cherry bomb explosive down the toilet.

'Wow! Can you say that again?' the male anchor exclaimed. 'You think somebody sent a cherry bomb down the toilet?'

'Yeah,' Mr Slungpue replied, adding, 'That's what coming back to us.

'We had a pressure break around sunset and the cherry bomb must have popped through the top of the pipe and just blew the bottom right out.'

Strange name: The joker called into ABC 7 in Los Angeles pretending to be a Department of Public Works employee named Louis Slungpue - which he noted should be pronounced 'Slung-poo'

Asked if he knew where the incident took place, Mr Slungpue said that the Department of Public Works is 'still in the investigation phase'.

The huge water main break left much of Sunset Boulevard and the UCLA campus under water Tuesday afternoon.

Water was gushing up to 30 feet into the air and some locals were trying to bodyboard in the water.

Officials from the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power later confirmed that a 93-year-old steel pipe broke at 3:39pm with an estimated 8 to 10 million gallons of water flowing toward the UCLA campus or into storm drains.

No one was reported injured and 160 firefighters responded to the scene. The cause still remains unknown.
