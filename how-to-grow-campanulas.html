How To Grow And Care For A Campanula Bellflower Plant

 Posted on August 13, 2024  |  5 minutes  |  1015 words  |  Larita Shotwell

Interested in growing campanula bellflower plants in your garden? Here, we ask Sue Wooster, from Bellflower Nursery at The Walled Garden in Suffolk, how to grow and care for campanula, the best varieties of campanula and where best to plant them in your garden.

What is a campanula bellflower plant?

Campanula, or bellflower plants, are colourful perennials and add cheer to any garden border. Alpine natives, campanula thrive in cool nights and moderate day temperatures and bloom from June to July – although some can flower all the way into October.

Their flowers are simple and pretty, particularly the forms with single hanging bells, such as the harebell and the peach-leaved bellflower, Campanula persicifolia.

The range of colours is magnificent, from purples, mauves, soft blues and pinks through to white. These hues look wonderful in herbaceous borders and blend well with a whole host of other hardy perennials.

Where do campanulas come from originally?

They grow solely in the northern hemisphere, predominantly throughout Europe, particularly in high places such as the European Alps and in Mediterranean countries.

The British Isles has a handful of native bellflowers such as the harebell (called 'bluebell' in Scotland), Campanula rotundifolia and C. patula – the spreading bellflower – which sadly only grows now in one or two pockets along the Welsh borders.

How to grow and care for a campanula plant: 7 tips

  • Alpine forms must have sharp drainage – add fine horticultural grit and sand to the soil or compost or they will suffer in winter wet.
  • They mostly prefer good light, too, so plant them in the open in full sun in a scree, rock garden or raised bed.
  • Clay pans are ideal but must have good drainage.
  • Border campanulas require sun for at least part of the day. One or two bellflowers work well in shade, such as Campanula latifolia (similar habit to a foxglove), particularly the white form, and Campanula rapunculoides 'Alba'.
  • Again, most prefer well-drained soil, but do benefit from an autumn or spring mulch of homemade compost or well-rotted manure.
  • Divide congested plants, whether alpine or border, on a regular basis.
  • The majority need neutral to limey conditions.

Can they be grown in containers?

Most of the dwarf campanulas are ideal. A selection of five or so in a clay pan or sink always works well. Choose one or two of the mat-forming varieties, such as Campanula cochlearifolia (fairy thimbles) and C. x wockei 'Puck', a taller form such as a white harebell or C. collina, and an edge-softener, for instance C. poscharskyana 'Blue Gown'.

Some border campanulas are also suited to containers. Traditionally, C. pyramidalis, the two-metre bellflower, would have been grown in huge clay pots and taken into the big house to stand in the empty fireplace over summer.

Which campanula varieties are best for a herbaceous border?

Campanula lactiflora (the milky bellflower) is really versatile. It has sprays of mid-blue starry flowers from late June and grows to 1.5m, although it also responds well to the Chelsea chop.

Cultivars are equally useful and magnificent at the back of the border – C. lactiflora 'Loddon Anna' is tall with masses of sprays of mid-pink stars. If you cut back as they fade, the flowering season can be extended into late summer. C. lactiflora 'Prichard's Variety' is one of the best, reaching 1.2m, with sprays of deep-blue star-shaped flowers with a white eye. It is quite hard to find the correct form of this, so try to buy it in flower if in any doubt.

Do campanula have any pest or disease problems?

Slugs and snails are rather partial to some of the dwarf alpine forms, so sharp grit and beer traps help keep them at bay. Orange-coloured rust on the undersides of foliage can affect some of the border campanulas.

If spotted, cut back the entire plant to ground level, cover with clean compost and fresh stems will emerge, generally rust-free.

A bit more about Sue Wooster and how she got into campanula gardening...

Have you always enjoyed gardening?

I had my own small patch when I was very young and my father and grandfather encouraged me to grow annuals and radishes. I remember being really excited at the age of seven when the first flowers of my purple petunias opened.

After I finished school, I studied languages at university and worked in London for a few years, so it wasn't until my sons were growing up that I began to cultivate my own cottage garden.

How long have you run the nursery?

The Walled Garden is a very special place and, until last year, it was privately owned by the Blackwell family. I met them in 2007 when I heard through a friend in the nursery trade that they were looking for someone to take on the garden.

Fortunately, the new owners are equally supportive.

When did you become interested in campanulas?

I first saw alpine campanulas growing in the Italian Dolomites when I lived there as a student in the 1980s. One or two really special bellflowers – Campanula raineri and C. morretiana – grow in this mountainous region, as well as many meadow forms such as the harebell, C. rotundifolia, and the bearded bellflower C. barbata.

Fifteen years ago, I studied horticulture part-time at a college near Ipswich and realised that raising plants was what I wanted to do as a profession. Coincidentally, I found a book on campanulas in the library written by Peter Lewis.

He and his wife Susan were the national collection holders at the time I contacted them to learn that they were retiring and closing their nursery in Cambridgeshire. They were delighted to find someone interested in growing the plants and I ended up establishing my own collection.
